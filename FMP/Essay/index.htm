What is the impact of how video games portray different urban forms?:

  • How does this express western/HIC biases?
  • In so, how does that affect people beliefs about how their urban areas could develop to more efficient, or sustainable forms?

This essay is justified on the premise that, firstly, video game’s representations have major ramifications on people's opinions which affect their actions affecting the world, and secondly, that financial and environmental crises that require changes in urban organisation. They will first be argued, then used as a framework to analyse how the video game industry’s output currently affects our urban crises.

It is well discussed that a game like “Uncharted: The Lost Legacy” (2017), even if its level of unrest and disunity is typical of any location in a video game’s representation, “it’s highly disappointing” (Deleted member 249, 2017) to many Indian gamers as it reinforces existing stereotypes about India while claiming its presentation is “being as authentic as we could” (Cooper, 2017). Gabbiadini et al. (2016) has also experimentally shown video games give players a level of empathy that is able to effect beliefs which have in turn been proven to be linked to violent acts, at least in the short term (which with modern live service and subscription based games as well as hobby style games as shall be defined later may make into a practically long-term effect through regular play sessions over years): They found that among 154 Italian high school students (aged 15-24) found strong correlations between playing violent and doubly so violent and sexist games and masculine ideas lacking female empathy which Gabbiadini et al. (2016) claim that Sanday (1981) shows are linked to violent acts against women. They found this moderation against gender (clearly women are else lightly to be led to struggle with female empathy, this is a limitation of the study as it lacked a mediator that effectively showed the effects on female participants) and self reported empathy with the characters in the games (which was found to be almost entirely proportional to the effects of the game on people's ideas). Beyond just negative correlations, they found games that weren’t sexist or violent without any actively progressive selection lead to a decrease in the harmful masculine ideas. Therefore, it would be reasonable to assume that, at least in games that incited a large deal of empathy, the presentation of the urban area’s the game takes place in can reinforce or incite questioning of the principles underlying our failing urban forms.

The approach used for considering what makes a good urban form on the accepted foundational text of The Transit Metropolis that Cervero (1998) wrote as historic public transit systems were failing around the world, analysing the few cities where “despite the fiscal bias against it” transit was thriving. He claimed “Automobiles completely destroy mass transit” and this lead to an unprecedented threat for “finite resources, natural landscapes, and economic wellbeing of our cities”; This he attributed to a political attitude of subsidisation’s effect of the real cost of parking and car ownership.

This we can see has only continued into this day with few cities reversing historic policies like parking minimums, now undeniably strong effect of climate change, and advocacy groups like the highly influential North American local-government advocacy group strong towns highlighting the more pressing financial problems of current top-down auto-oriented urban areas leading to chronic town bankruptcies and austerity; This can easily be argued to be the primary cause of the increase in US local-government debt from 9% to 13% 2000-2020 (Statista, 2024), (St Louis Fed, 2024); This even with the combined subsidies of an average of 65% by state and federal governments still continuing and end use subsidies such as the many global new energy vehicles subsidy programs and free carbon emissions while institutional polluter are charged). The US’s development style is still seen as a target for many developing countries, and so its problems are likely to occur amongst most globalised countries in the future. Additionally, programs like Saudi Arabia’s Oil Demand Sustainability Program which includes “A sophisticated strategy to deploy fleets of petrol and diesel-fuelled vehicles across Asia and Africa'' (Lawrence, 2023) have created a supergovernment equivalent of the US’ federal highway construction funding that left many American cities with road’s they can’t afford to maintain, “a Greek gift that will further sink Africa into climate catastrophe” (Lawrence, 2023).

Transportation is such an important factor discussed for cities as it both globally considered what people care about most in a city (citation needed) and in auto-dependent cities such as games typically show they take between 35% and 50% of all land-use (8.2 – Urban Land Use and Transportation | The Geography of Transport Systems, 2024).

Cervero (1998) specifies the city that remained sustainable as a ‘Transit Metropolis’ (“a region where a workable fit between transit services & urban form exists.”) are “highly adaptive” relying on local solutions to become either “adapted cities'' which adapt the urban form to be conducive to transit, have “adaptive transit” which is uniquely able to work within that city sprawl pattern and compete against the every growing curse of motor traffic and allow for the phasing out of subsidies, or hybrids that manage both to a moderate amount they can manage. Because of this need for local adaptations against national and supranational pressures means that individuals seeing the possibility and wishing to fight for its reality locally is an effective way of realising the change we need. Therefore, lay people’s games are some of the biggest influences of the future of our cities.

AAA Open-world Action-Adventure CRPGs (Anecdotally the vast majority of AAA games with character empathy as was previously shown to be necessary to affect opinions), due to their large teams making custom assets, are least limited by technical limitations on diversity, organicity, or complexity. They do however exist in a world that designed for the freedom and usually “designed for violence” (Jacob Geller, 2019) too of a single or few players; To achieve this games usually through a system of theft (e.g. GTA V) or sci-fi technology (e.g. Cyberpunk 2077) allow the freedom of driving without the disadvantages of parking; They also normalise the violent harm of unsafe driving. The only other system that’s become a standard, while being near universally seen as a passible but imperfect solution, is a non-skeuomorphic menu and bland loading screen.

However, many games regardless of age have experimented with using different ways to make public and self-propelled transit a key and engaging game mechanic. A basic implementation of public transit is in Cyberpunk 2077's latest update, where its metro system was made functional as a fast travel option with a cutscene allowing seeing the city as you travel. More traditional CRPGs like “Elder Scrolls: Oblivion”, having 4 different transport systems linked to other game mechanics with connectivity between different areas, engaging the player through making transportation a choice engaging with the game's mechanics rather than just a driving minigame to play within the world of the game they intend to play. This same divide occurs in our actual urban areas, but a game helps make this more evident.

Self-propelled transport is especially seen by traditional urbanists like Jane Jacobs to allow for the most ‘human-oriented’ urban form, this becomes geometrically impossible in large urban areas necessary for highly specialised economies and diverse cultures; Games however are required even with AAA budgets to create the idea of large areas in smaller ones making them more suited to such transport. This too has been recognised by the game industry with many narrative games now referred to as ‘walking simulators’ where the act of walking is a key mechanic to space and allows for human empathy with the setting. More action focused games (which hold most of the market) tend to try and give the players more rather than less freedom to experience the setting, such as climbing mechanics popularised in the Assassin's Creed series allowing one to explore and appreciate the city’s verticality, crucial to its real life success. Spider-Man (2018), the fastest-selling first-party video game release in Sony's history, makes travel it’s selling feature with a dramatised and story-adaptive version of New York navigated by swinging from the tops of building, requiring the player to learn the cities urban layout and act in literal rhythm with its scale; This also ties long distance movement into the same mechanics used in the rest of the gameplay, attaching it to people's emotional attachment to the protagonist that was shown to be necessary to affect people's thinking patterns.

Another important factor is the architectural arrangement of buildings and urban areas, be it a classical garden city or modernist polycentric auto-sprawl. Even in the most auto-oriented cities and games at least 50% of the land is taken by buildings and the open spaces they stand in and are framed by; in any city the density and arrangement of it’s building leads to the demand for travel, and in so it’s land use; additionally, in a classical city the buildings are constrained by the reality of the cities roads, this is not the case with modernism. When detaching buildings from classical road modernisms aims to allowing better access to, views, and light from park space, this is shared by garden cities who wished to do a similar idea, though often the opposite of its original aims is achieved in reality through an integrated garden suburb, but on a larger scale with buildings attached to roads but those roads are built of a shared green space separated from the energy of transport arteries rather than it being the gaps left in between them. The one other aspect used in early modernism (e.g. barbican) is ‘streets in the sky’, the idea of vertically separating pedestrian movement from leisure and vehicular traffic, dating back in theory as far back as the plans for Barcelona [citation needed].

This demand for separation and contrast in cities, AAA OAACRPGs deliver well on; Firstly, map size constraints force density to be used in any wilder setting is wanted in conjunction with the city; generally this transition between wilderness and urban in such a short space provides a lot of joy to the otherwise dull travel mechanics. Secondly, a lack of respect for rules and risks and I need to avoid enforcement mean the full verticality of cities to be used from the many subway markets and sky street of Cyberpunk 2077 to the franchise defining climbing mechanics in the historical settings of the assassins creed games, unintentional ‘walking simulators’. This makes classical development relatively rare. Even in a realistic-aiming depiction of a classical city like GTA VI show the sharp cut of density from tightly packed classical small houses to mountains that is the product of southern California’s landscape in conjunction with endless sprawl that leads to it’s surprisingly leading density, only with shrunken zones and overall city size meaning it achieves the garden city dream while having a relatable aesthetic of a typical planned auto-oriented American city.

Aside from the largest games, city builders in their many genre-crossing forms provide the largest though possibly most wasted opportunity. Most of the genre to a fault attempts to build on Will Wright’s SimCity (1989). The early 90s were a much more diverse time to develop games and software, with very few standards on things such as menus [All The Widgets" CHI'90 Special Issue #57 ACM SIGGRAPH Video Review] with almost every part of gameplay and UI having to be uniquely engineered on by every game and for every platform (26 for SimCity [SimCity (1989 video game) - Wikipedia.[January 24, 2024]] ); Therefore many bold decisions made by Wright when “just kind optimis(ing) for gameplay” off the base of a RTS level editor. It was also built off contemporary established American planning ideas with lots of links to the existing administrative apparatus (“CIA, Defense Department, Canadian Lumber association …” all were in talks over specialist versions at ~650,0002023USD each). Other games released at the time have created similar fixed markets, such as A-Train (1985) into its Sequels, and Transport Tycoon (1994) into the Transport Fever series. Most importantly these games are designed more as entire “hobbies rather than games” [Will Wright] only attractive to new players happy with “captivating confusion” [Clint Basinger]. The uncertain difficulty of putting new players through a difficult learning curve in a much more competitive game market and the fact that its practices were literally included in university classes has left games teaching flawed and outdated ideas of urban planning, neither relatable, engaging or educational. Innovation from the much cheaper to experiment with and fast to learn board games is what lead to an varied market of abstract puzzle style city building games, though most still feel too abstracted to be able to generate the required empathy. Should this approachability be combined with more realism and modern academic beliefs it could teach the principles of good cities at a deep level to many people.

Conclusion

In this essay I established the power, and with that responsibility, of games to influence the form of our cities. I don’t feel I found a gap in the mainstream AAA and AAA-like game market I could contribute effectively to; Instead I found that the diverse genre of city builders have remained stagnant in the wake of innovations in urban academia and game design; I shall make an functional prototype of such a game with an interest to build it into a full game should it be successful.
